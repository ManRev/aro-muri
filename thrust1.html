Choice-set models under computational constraints

Learning contextual choice behavior from observational data

Choices lie at the heart of the most influential actions people take: voting choices determine our elected officials, online choices govern the success of recommender systems, and social choices shape our friend networks. By analyzing choice data, we can discover behavioral patterns that underlie these choices, enabling better intervention design and improving our ability to predict future choices. There is a wealth of web-scale observational choice datasets, but analyzing them requires both flexible models capable of capturing subtle behavioral effects and robust causal inference techniques to avoid confounding factors. Additionally, there is a strong social component to many choices people make, prompting joint analysis of social network and choice data.

In this line of research, we have developed interpretable choice models that allow the discovery of feature-level contextual interactions from choice data. In addition to being easy to train, these models are derived from simple assumptions, have easily interpretable parameters, and are amenable to theoretical analysis. In the causal inference direction, we have identified conditions under which discrete choice models trained from observational data are susceptible to confounding (e.g., finding context effects where there are none or learning skewed preferences). We have also adapted causal inference methods for deconfounding choice models, in some cases allowing provably correct estimation from observational data. Finally, we are developing methods for using social or geographical networks to improve choice predictions.

Our models for feature context effects reveal a variety of novel interactionsâ€”for instance, we find in a hotel booking dataset that people shown sets of hotels with higher star rating are willing to pay for more expensive hotels. We also perform the first analysis of context effects in online social network growth, finding across many datasets that users forming connections place relatively more emphasis on shared neighbors when popular users are an option. Using our causal inference methods, we find that the apparent context effects in some widely-used benchmark datasets are actually the result of confounding. We also show that we can take advantage of confounding in some cases to identify the components of a heterogeneous population and use this to improve choice prediction.
